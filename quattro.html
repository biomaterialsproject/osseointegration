L: Good morning everyone, the first definition was given by Dr. Branemark who described osseointegration as: “a direct contact- on light microscopic level- between living bone and implant”.
L: In general, we can say that osteointegration is the biological process through which a medical implant directly anchorage by the formation of bony tissue around it without growth of fibrous tissue at the interface between the bone and implant.
L: Actually, the definition of osseointegration has slightly changed through the years and even now researchers continue to give different interpretations of the term.
C: Why is it so difficult to find a unique definition?
L: Currently, it is difficult to say how good the anchorage of the implant is because the existing techniques [ref. episode 3] can't provide detailed information about the interface between the bone and the implant.
L: Moreover, we can approach osseointegration from different points of view: First, as the absence of motion between the implant and surrounding tissues. Second, as the growth of new, re-formed bone in congruence with the implant, without fibrous tissue. Finally, at microscopic levels, where tissue components near the implant are identified as normal bone and marrow constituents.
C: So, nowadays, is there a definition that is considered the most comprehensive?
L: There is no definitive answer, but research is increasingly moving towards the detailed study of structures at the micrometric and nanometric levels that interoperate to better understand how osteointegration takes place and to be able to give a more specific definition. Today, to consider an implant as osseointegrated at a later stage after implantation, it must meet two fundamental criteria: The bone-implant interface should primarily consist of highly ordered mineralized collagen fibrils. Osteocytes be able to form and maintain a physical connection with the implant surface.

Episode 5: Failure and Challenges

TRANSCRIPTION
L: Hi there! We're back with a new episode of our podcast. In the previous episodes, we explored methodologies to improve cell adhesion to the implant surface and techniques for visualizing the bone-implant interface.
L: Unfortunately, osseointegration isn’t always successful, and when it fails, the implant can lose its connection with the bone. What are the reasons behind this? We will discover some more about this topic with (nome).
C: You’re right, even though a correct osseointegration of devices has a high success rate, failures can still occur and lead to implant loss or complications requiring revision surgery.
L: Which factors are mostly involved?
C: There are several elements that make osseointegration more challenging including biological, mechanical and surgical issues. When it comes to biological factors, the general health of the patient and infections are the leading cause of improper osseointegration, for example, conditions like osteoporosis affect the bone quality and reduces the chances of osseointegration. Mechanical factors are mainly related to implant stability. In particular, lack of osseointegration is usually accompanied by micro-movement of the implant in his location. Another factor that can incur is the excessive mechanical load that can lead to microfractures of the bone and failure, especially if it is applied right after implantation. Lastly, surgical techniques are important to ensure proper osseointegration both because they guarantee primary stability and help to prevent infections.
L: When is it useful to evaluate osseointegration?
C: Actually, in recent years, we have come to understand the importance of having a continuous follow-up to keep evaluating device osseointegration throughout its life. In fact, osseointegration is a dynamic process that changes over time, so regular monitoring helps detect issues early, preventing complications like implant loosening or failure.
L: Before we wrap up, are there any advancements or future trends in implant technology or osseointegration evaluation that could help prevent these failures?
C: Yes, absolutely. Advances in imaging technologies, such as micro-CT scans and 3D imaging, have significantly improved our ability to monitor the bone-implant interface. These tools allow us to track subtle changes in real time, which can help us intervene before any issues become critical. Moreover, by monitoring the biological processes involved in osseointegration, we can better understand how the bone and implant are interacting, potentially leading to more personalized treatment approaches and better long-term results. At the same time, improved materials and surface modifications are making integration more effective and implants more stable.
L: Thank you for your time. We are almost at the end of our journey, don’t miss our last episode! See you soon!
